The Rarity Codex

How Yu-Gi-Oh!, Magic: The Gathering, and Pokémon engineer scarcity, desire, and the chase.

Design Insight Yu-Gi-Oh is unique in permanently binding gameplay identity to physical treatment. A card reprinted at a lower rarity is a genuinely different object — it looks different, feels different, and commands a different market price. This creates a powerful secondary collector market entirely separate from competitive play demand.

Design Insight Magic's separation of rarity from visual treatment gives it the most flexible reprint policy of the three games. The same card can be reprinted at any rarity, in any frame, in any foil treatment — without changing its gameplay identity. This has proven essential for keeping competitive formats accessible while maintaining a robust collector tier above it.

Modern Hit Slots — Scarlet & Violet Era

Holo Rare / Double Rare (ex)
Holo pattern in the art window. Double Rare applies to all ex-mechanic cards — the standard "pull" in modern sets.
~1 in 3 packs
Reverse Holo
Holo foil applied to everything outside the art window (border, text box, energy cost). Every single pack contains exactly one.
1 per pack (guaranteed)
Illustration Rare (IR)
Art bleeds past the inner frame border into the text box area. A scene rather than a portrait. The "entry-level" art chase card.
~1 in 18 packs
Special Illustration Rare (SIR)
Full illustrated background wrapping the Pokémon into a scene. The card border disappears. Drives the modern collector market — same gameplay as Double Rare.
~1 in 50–80 packs
Hyper Rare / ACE SPEC
The entire card — art, frame, text, energy symbols — printed in gold foil. One ACE SPEC is guaranteed per booster box (36 packs).
~1 in 70–100 packs

Design Insight Pokémon's genius move in the modern era was decoupling collector value from competitive value entirely. An SIR Charizard ex and a Double Rare Charizard ex are the same card in a tournament — but worlds apart in a binder. This lets the game serve both audiences without compromising either. The guaranteed reverse holo ensures every pack opener walks away with something shiny, dramatically reducing buyer regret at the point of sale.

Great Guy Games LLC · 2–6 Players · Ages 7+ · 58-card base deck · 12-card expansions

Payback Card Game

Payback is a competitive card-shedding game for 2–6 players. Each player starts with 9 cards: 3 face-down (never peeked), 3 face-up on top of them, and 3 in hand. The goal is to get rid of all your cards across three phases — hand cards first, then face-up, then the blind face-down flip. Play equal or higher to the pile, or unleash one of 5 special cards (Ghost, Skip, Mirror, Reset, Burn) at any time. Four-of-a-kind burns the pile and resets play. If you can't beat the pile, you pick it up. First out wins; last player standing loses. The 12-card Dark Wizard expansion adds spell cards that run in parallel — cast before your turn to sabotage rivals, bend the rules, or pull off impossible comebacks. Because Payback sells complete decks and complete expansion sets with no randomness, "rarity" here is about premium edition variants, visual treatments, and numbered print runs — not pull rates.

Choose a Rarity System for Payback

Option A
The Foil Deck Model

You sell the base game in two physical versions — Standard and Foil Edition. Every expansion (all 12 cards) automatically ships with a premium foil treatment — buyers always get the complete set. Simple, no randomness, easy to manufacture. You're already doing a version of this with Original / Degen / Gold.

Advantages

Zero randomness — customers know exactly what they're buying. Simple SKU structure. Easy to manufacture (just two print runs per base set). Works perfectly with your current Gold Edition model. Expansions feel inherently special.

Tradeoffs

No per-card collectibility — you can't chase a specific card. Limited secondary market. Once someone owns the Foil Edition, there's no next tier to want. Doesn't create the "opening experience" excitement.

Option B
The Within-Expansion Split

The base deck stays fully standard. Inside each 12-card expansion, cards are printed at two treatment levels — most are standard, but a designated subset (the mechanic cards, wildcards, or thematic "anchors") ship in foil. Every buyer gets all 12 cards and knows exactly what they're getting. Rarity is built into the expansion's internal structure.

Advantages

Zero randomness — buyers know the exact treatment of every card before purchasing. Creates a visual hierarchy within the expansion itself that signals which cards are most important. The foil anchor cards become iconic at the table. Collector Edition creates a premium tier without requiring a separate product line.

Tradeoffs

Requires deciding which 3 cards are "anchor" cards per expansion — a design decision, not just a manufacturing one. Two print specs per expansion (foil + standard) adds manufacturing complexity. Collector Edition needs a separate numbered print run coordination with Flydragon.

Option C
The Expansion Tier Model

Keep the base game fully standardized. Build a treatment tier system entirely around expansions — each of the 12 cards ships in the same edition, but you offer Standard, Foil, and Gold Numbered versions of the complete expansion. Buyers choose their tier upfront. The base game stays clean; the collector market lives in the expansions.

Advantages

The base game stays 100% fair — no collector advantage bleeds into gameplay. Concentrates all collectibility in expansions where you can control print quantities precisely. The Gold /200 creates genuine scarcity events around each expansion launch. Works naturally with your Dark Wizard, NSFW, Mystery structure.

Tradeoffs

Requires 3–4 SKUs per expansion instead of one. Buyers who want the Gold edition need to act fast (which is also a pro). The alternate art requires a commissioned illustration budget per expansion. More complex manufacturing coordination with Flydragon.
